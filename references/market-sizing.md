# Market Sizing — Gurley Bottom-Up Methodology

## Why Top-Down TAM/SAM/SOM Fails

"The global X market is $100B, we need 1% to be huge."

This tells an investor nothing about how the business will actually grow. Every step involves an invented assumption. The resulting number sounds rigorous but is functionally made up. Founders who present top-down TAM are revealing they haven't thought through how they will acquire customers.

## The Bottom-Up Method — Step by Step

### Step 1 — Define the Specific Customer Unit

Not "mid-market HR companies." Instead:
> "US companies with 100-500 employees that have an HR team of 3-10 people, are currently on a legacy system (Paycom, ADP), and are owned by a PE firm that is pushing operational efficiency."

The more specific the definition, the more credible the count.

### Step 2 — Count Them

Use real tools and sources:
- LinkedIn Sales Navigator (filter by title, company size, industry, technology used)
- ZoomInfo / Apollo estimates
- Industry association membership numbers
- Trade conference attendance figures
- Competitor customer lists (LinkedIn, G2, Capterra reviews)

If you can't count the customers, you don't know the market.

### Step 3 — Estimate Realistic Reachability in Year 1

Of the universe identified:
- How many can you reach through your specific distribution channel?
- What conversion rate is realistic given your current stage, brand, and product maturity?
- How many sales cycles can you run in 12 months with current team capacity?

This produces a **Year 1 achievable customer count** — not an aspiration, a constraint.

### Step 4 — Model Revenue Per Customer

- Realistic ACV (Annual Contract Value) — based on what similar products charge, not what you wish you could charge
- Realistic retention / churn
- Net Revenue Retention (are customers expanding? Contracting?)

**Year 1 ARR = reachable customers × conversion rate × ACV**

### Step 5 — Project Forward and Sanity-Check

Model Years 1-3 with conservative, base, and optimistic assumptions. Then compare to top-down TAM as a sanity check:

- If bottom-up suggests $500M ARR ceiling but top-down TAM is $200M — an assumption is wrong
- If bottom-up Year 3 is $5M ARR but you're pitching a $10B opportunity — the path doesn't connect

## The "Market You Can Win" Question (Gurley)

The correct question is not "how large is the market?" but:

> "What is the subset of the market where our specific solution creates so much value that we can achieve dominant share? What does that subset look like and what is its value?"

A $50B market where you can realistically capture 0.01% is worse than a $500M market where you can capture 30%.

## The Adjacent Market Trap

Founders inflate TAM by including markets they "might enter eventually." This is harmful:
1. Signals to investors that the plan isn't specific
2. Misleads the founder about the actual near-term opportunity

Correct framing:
> "Our initial market is X ($Ym ARR addressable). If we dominate X, the adjacencies that become reachable are A and B, adding $Z over time."

## Beachhead Sizing (Moore)

The beachhead must be:
- Small enough to dominate with 18 months of focused effort
- Large enough that dominance creates a meaningful bowling pin effect into adjacent segments
- Specific enough that every player in it can be named or contacted

Rule of thumb: if you can't describe a path to 30%+ market share in the beachhead within 24 months, the beachhead is either too large or the distribution channel is wrong.
